The Experience Machine

In his essay “The Experience Machine”, Robert Nozick explores the question of whether or not people would use a life experience simulation machine if such a thing existed. He argues that even if the machine could perfectly emulate any experience one desired, people would still not use it to replace their daily lives because humans crave the reality of life. Even if the experience machine could be fully immersive to the extent that the user believes it’s actually happening, Nozick believes that as people we seek to actually do things rather than only live the experience of them. He also points out that a reason that we would not choose this alternative reality is because we could only experience what humans have designed. Limiting ourselves to a man-made world would inherently rid ourselves of a true connection to nature or anything outside the scope of our own imaginations and capabilities. Nozick then discusses how the use of psychoactive substances can be seen as either a sort of local experience machine or as an “avenue to a deeper reality”. He claims that this conflict is clarified by the idea that “There is no actual contact with any deeper reality, though the experience of it can be simulated. Many persons desire to leave themselves open to such contact and to a plumbing of deeper significance” (Nozick). Nozick then extends his argument that people would not use the experience machine to the claim that people would also not use a hypothetical transformation machine. Even if a machine could allow us to be whatever person we’d like to be while still being ourselves, Nozick believes that we would not use it. His argument is based on the idea that we would be too removed from our true sense of self and reality if our experiences and selves were a simulation, and so our lives would not be satisfying in the way they currently are. He reaches the conclusion that “perhaps what we desire is to live (an active verb) ourselves, in contact with reality. (And this, machines cannot do for us)” (Nozick).

In my own opinion, I agree that a life of simulated experiences and false identity would be less satisfying and rewarding than one that is engaged in reality. However, I am not convinced that we as a society would not take the opportunity to simulate our lives if we could. The 2011 science fiction novel Ready Player One by Ernest Cline explores this idea of a world in which humans live in a virtual reality and experience everything, including relationships and all interactions, through a computer and VR headset. After reading this book, I am inclined to agree with Cline that this is a fairly plausible future, as we have already taken certain steps in this direction since the invention of the VR experience in day-to-day life (in entertainment such as gaming, as well as media consumption such as movies and even pornography). Taking into consideration that Nozick’s essay was published in 1975, far before the advent of smart devices, let alone VR machines, it is understandable that for Nozick it was much harder to imagine a world in which people would actually subscribe to the “experience machine”. In our modern society, however, it is really not so far-fetched to argue that perhaps we would use the experience machine if we had the chance to.
